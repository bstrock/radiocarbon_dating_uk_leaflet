In this map, you're invited to explore the prehistory of the UK after the Younger Dryas.

Each dot represents an organic sample which has been radiocarbon dated. As the time sequence changes, the size of the dot will expand into a circle based on the probability the sample originated in that year.

The Younger Dryas

13,000 years ago, most of the Northern hemisphere froze for centuries during the Younger Dryas (12,900-11,700 BCE).

This was a very different world than ours– the waters of today's English Channel and North Sea were locked in massive glaciers, and much of today's UK was uninhabitable.

Radiocarbon Probabilities

New methodologies leverage radiocarbon data through advanced probability modeling.

These techniques can illuminate the migration, growth, and expansion patterns of ancient populations by gathering large volumes of radiocarbon sample data and analyzing the frequency of individual radiocarbon dates.

Time Slider and Controls

Drag the red circle to sweep through the time series. Each step represents 5 years.

Playback controls offer automatic sequencing. Changing the speed control increases the step interval: time passes faster, but at the cost of decreased detail!

Combine playback and manual manipulation to explore the data.

Symbology

Many sites contain more than one artifact. Symbols are semi-transparent: brighter circles indicate more higher-probability samples in one area.

The controls on the right side change symbol color according to the sample's associated archaeological culture. Not all samples are associated with a culture.

Archaeological Cultures
Bell Beaker pottery vessel. Credit: Natural History Museum of London
Archaeological cultures describe common elements found in the material remains of ancient population groups. While cultures can be distinctive, these labels can also obscure underlying processes of change within groups, as well as important differences between them.

Explore and Interpret

This tool is designed for data exploration.

Every aspect of these analyses are controversial: while radiocarbon probabilities may offer insight into population growth trends, these data may only describe where we've done archaeological research, or when environmental conditions were suitable for preservation.

Every user should carefully interrogate these data, and cross-reference against other sources (Ancient DNA, the material record, etc.) whenever possible.
